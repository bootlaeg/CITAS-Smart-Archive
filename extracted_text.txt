Mindanao Institute of Technology and Applied Sciences June 2024 ABSTRACT Neurological disorders, including Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, and epilepsy, represent a growing global health crisis, affecting hundreds of millions of individuals worldwide and placing unsustainable burdens on healthcare systems. Early and accurate detection remains the most effective strategy for improving patient outcomes; however, existing diagnostic tools demand specialized expertise, expensive equipment, and time-intensive procedures that are largely inaccessible in low-resource settings, particularly in the rural Philippines. This study presents  NeuroGuard , an intelligent, multi-modal diagnostic support system that integrates deep learning-based analysis of electroencephalographic (EEG) signals, gait motion data captured via wearable inertial measurement units, and natural language cognitive assessment transcripts.  NeuroGuard  employs a custom Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer (MTFT) architecture that achieves inter-modal attention across asynchronous  biosignal  streams, enabling robust classification of neurological status with minimal latency. The system was developed following the Agile Software Development Lifecycle and validated against a dataset of 1,240 anonymized patient records sourced from three partnering regional hospitals. Experimental results demonstrate that  NeuroGuard  attains an overall classification accuracy of 94.7%, a sensitivity of 96.2%, and a specificity of 93.1% across six diagnostic categories. Furthermore, the system's web-based dashboard and lightweight mobile companion application enable real-time monitoring and longitudinal tracking by both clinicians and patients, significantly reducing the diagnostic turnaround from an average of 14.3 days to 2.1 hours in simulated clinical workflows. Usability evaluation conducted with 45 healthcare professionals yielded a System Usability Scale (SUS) score of 82.4, indicating high acceptability. These findings suggest that  NeuroGuard  holds substantial promise as a scalable, cost-effective adjunct to traditional neurological diagnosis, especially in resource-constrained environments. with its intricate network of approximately 86 billion neurons and trillions of synaptic connections, constitutes the most complex biological structure known to science. Its functional integrity underpins every facet of human experience, from cognition, memory, and language to motor control, sensory perception, and emotional regulation. When the structural or biochemical architecture of the nervous system is disrupted, the resulting neurological disorders impose devastating consequences not only on the individual but on families, communities, and healthcare systems at large. According to the World Health Organization (2023), neurological conditions are now the leading cause of disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) globally, surpassing cardiovascular disease and cancer in terms of years lived with disability. Among the most clinically significant neurological conditions are Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, and ischemic stroke. Parkinson's disease alone affects an estimated 8.5 million people worldwide, with incidence rates doubling in the past 25 years owing to aging populations and, to a lesser extent, environmental factors. Alzheimer's disease, the most common cause of dementia, afflicts over 55 million individuals globally, with projections suggesting this figure will triple to 153 million by 2050 in the absence of transformative intervention. Epilepsy, characterized by recurrent unprovoked seizures, affects approximately 50 million people across all demographic groups, making it the fourth most common neurological disorder after migraine, stroke, and dementia. The diagnostic landscape for neurological disorders has historically been characterized by complexity, cost, and inequity. The gold standard for many conditions, including epilepsy and sleep disorders, remains the electroencephalogram (EEG), a non-invasive neuroimaging technique that records the brain's electrical activity through scalp electrodes. While EEG provides rich temporal information at millisecond resolution, its interpretation demands years of specialist training. Neurologists and  epileptologists  must visually examine multi-channel  waveforms spanning hours of recording, a process that is inherently subjective, time-consuming, and prone to inter-rater variability. In high-income countries with robust specialist workforces, this bottleneck is manageable, though still significant. In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), the situation is acute: the global neurologist-to-patient ratio is approximately 0.1 per 100,000 in the lowest-income settings, compared to 10 per 100,000 in high-income countries. The Philippines presents a compelling and sobering case study of this diagnostic disparity. With a population exceeding 115 million distributed across over 7,000 islands, neurological healthcare services are overwhelmingly concentrated in Metro Manila and a handful of regional urban centers. The Philippine Neurological Association estimates that fewer than 600 board-certified neurologists serve the entire country, yielding a specialist ratio of approximately 0.52 per 100,000, well below the WHO-recommended threshold of 1 per 100,000. Patients in Mindanao, the Visayas, and rural Luzon often travel hundreds of kilometers and wait weeks or months to access specialist evaluation. When they finally receive assessment, the cost of an EEG study, neuropsychological testing, and specialist consultation can range from PHP 8,000 to PHP 25,000, an amount that exceeds the monthly income of a significant portion of Filipino households. Compounding this, diagnostic equipment in many provincial hospitals is outdated, poorly maintained, or outright absent. In this context, the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) into neurological diagnostics represents not merely a technological advancement but a potentially transformative intervention with profound social equity implications. Over the past decade, deep learning models, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and more recently, attention-based Transformer architectures, have demonstrated remarkable performance in medical image analysis, time-series classification, and natural language understanding. Studies published in journals such as Nature Medicine, The Lancet Neurology, and the Journal of Neural Engineering have reported AI systems achieving specialist-level or super-specialist-level diagnostic accuracy across multiple neurological conditions when validated on sufficiently large and representative datasets. However, the vast majority of existing AI-driven neurological diagnostic tools focus on a single data modality, relying solely on EEG signals, MRI scans, or clinical interview data.  This unimodal approach, while computationally tractable, fails to capture the full clinical picture. Neurological disorders are inherently multidimensional phenomena. Parkinson's disease, for instance, manifests not only in cortical electrical patterns but in characteristic gait abnormalities (reduced stride length, shuffling, freezing of gait), subtle changes in speech prosody, and declining performance on visuospatial and executive function tests. A diagnostic tool that analyzes only EEG signals will inevitably miss early-stage Parkinson's disease, where cortical changes are subtle, while motor and behavioral signatures are already measurable. Conversely, a gait-only system cannot distinguish between Parkinson's, normal pressure hydrocephalus, and vascular parkinsonism without additional biomarkers. The concept of multimodal fusion, integrating information from multiple heterogeneous data sources into a unified representation, has emerged as the frontier of clinical AI research. Inspired by the holistic assessment approach of expert clinicians, who simultaneously consider patient history, physical examination findings, imaging results, and laboratory data, multimodal AI systems promise to substantially outperform single-modality baselines. The challenge lies in the technical complexity of aligning, weighting, and fusing asynchronous data streams of different dimensionalities and temporal resolutions, a challenge for which the emerging field of multimodal deep learning is developing increasingly powerful solutions. It is against this backdrop that the present study was conceived. The researchers propose  NeuroGuard , a comprehensive AI-powered system for early detection and longitudinal monitoring of neurological disorders, designed specifically for deployment in low-resource clinical environments in the Philippines. By integrating EEG analysis, wearable-sensor gait analysis, and natural language cognitive assessment within a novel Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer (MTFT) architecture,  NeuroGuard  aims to deliver specialist-grade diagnostic support at a fraction of the cost and time of traditional methods. The system encompasses a clinician-facing web dashboard for hospital settings and a lightweight mobile application for remote monitoring, together forming a complete, deployable solution for bridging the neurological care gap. 1.2 Statement of the Problem Despite significant advances in both neuroscience and artificial intelligence, the translation of these advances into accessible, practical diagnostic tools for neurological disorders remains profoundly inadequate, particularly in the Philippine healthcare context. The central problem this study addresses is the absence of an integrated, AI-driven, multimodal diagnostic and monitoring system capable of supporting the early detection of neurological disorders in resource-constrained clinical environments. More specifically, the study seeks to address the following subsidiary problems: First, existing neurological diagnostic procedures are prohibitively expensive, time-intensive, and specialist-dependent, rendering them inaccessible to a large proportion of the Filipino population, particularly those in rural and geographically isolated communities. The consequence is widespread late-stage diagnosis, at which point treatment options are substantially reduced and prognosis is significantly worsened. Second, no single diagnostic modality provides sufficient sensitivity and specificity to reliably distinguish among the six target neurological conditions under investigation in this study, namely Parkinson's disease, early-stage Alzheimer's disease, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, post-stroke cognitive impairment, and healthy control status. Unimodal systems, while demonstrated to be effective for specific conditions in controlled research settings, lack the clinical robustness necessary for deployment as general neurological screening tools. Third, there is a critical absence of longitudinal monitoring infrastructure for neurological patients in the Philippine public health system. Once diagnosed, patients receive periodic clinic-based assessments separated by intervals of months, during which disease progression, medication side effects, or early complications may go undetected. A continuous or semi-continuous monitoring solution integrated with the diagnostic system is necessary but lacking. Fourth, existing AI-based medical diagnostic tools developed in high-income country contexts have rarely been validated on Filipino patient populations, raising serious questions about their generalizability given known differences in genetic predispositions, comorbidity profiles, dietary factors, and socioeconomic determinants of health. A locally validated system is therefore not merely preferable but essential. 1.3 Objectives of the Study The primary objective of this study is to design, develop, and evaluate  NeuroGuard , an AI-powered multimodal system for the early detection and continuous monitoring of neurological disorders, validated on a Philippine patient dataset. To achieve this overarching goal, the following specific objectives were pursued: To design and implement a Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer (MTFT) architecture capable of integrating EEG signal data, wearable inertial measurement unit (IMU) gait data, and natural language cognitive assessment transcripts into a unified diagnostic classification framework. To develop a comprehensive data preprocessing and feature extraction pipeline for each of the three input modalities, including artifact rejection and spectral decomposition for EEG, stride segmentation and kinematic feature extraction for gait data, and transformer-based embedding generation for clinical transcripts. To validate the classification performance of  NeuroGuard  on a dataset of 1,240 anonymized patient records across six diagnostic categories, using standard performance metrics including accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, F1-score, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC). To develop and deploy a clinician-facing web-based dashboard enabling real-time diagnostic reporting, patient record management, and longitudinal trend visualization. To develop and deploy a lightweight mobile companion application for patient-side data collection and self-monitoring, compatible with low-end Android devices prevalent in the Philippine market. To evaluate the usability and acceptability of  NeuroGuard  among healthcare professionals using the System Usability Scale (SUS) and qualitative feedback collection. To assess the impact of  NeuroGuard  on clinical diagnostic workflow efficiency, specifically the reduction in time-to-diagnosis and specialist consultation requirements. 1.4 Significance of the Study The significance of this study extends across multiple dimensions, encompassing clinical, technological, socioeconomic, and academic spheres. Each is elaborated below. From a clinical perspective,  NeuroGuard  directly addresses a critical gap in neurological care delivery in the Philippines by providing a validated, AI-augmented diagnostic support tool that can function with minimal specialist involvement. The system's ability to analyze multimodal  biosignals  and generate clinically interpretable diagnostic reports  has the potential to substantially reduce diagnostic delays for patients in underserved regions, thereby enabling earlier therapeutic intervention and improved long-term outcomes. For patients suffering from conditions such as Parkinson's disease or Alzheimer's disease, where disease-modifying interventions are most effective in early stages, even a modest reduction in diagnostic latency could translate into meaningful improvements in quality of life and functional independence. From a technological perspective, the MTFT architecture proposed in this study represents a novel contribution to the field of multimodal medical AI. Unlike existing fusion approaches that rely on fixed-weight concatenation or simple ensemble voting, the MTFT employs dynamic, learned cross-modal attention mechanisms that adaptively weight each modality's contribution based on signal quality, patient demographic factors, and temporal context. This architecture is generalizable beyond neurological disorders and may inform future research in cardiovascular monitoring, oncological diagnosis, and psychiatric assessment, among other domains. From a socioeconomic perspective,  NeuroGuard  has the potential to democratize access to high-quality neurological diagnostic support by dramatically reducing the cost barrier. The system is designed to operate on commodity hardware, utilizing cloud-based inference where available and on-device inference for offline functionality, ensuring accessibility even in areas with unreliable internet connectivity. If scaled nationally through integration with PhilHealth's primary care benefit package or the Department of Health's telemedicine initiative,  NeuroGuard  could reach millions of Filipinos currently excluded from specialist neurological care. For the academic community, this study contributes a rigorously curated, annotated dataset of Filipino neurological patient  biosignals , the first of its kind in the published literature, which will be made available through a controlled data-sharing agreement to facilitate future research. Additionally, the methodological framework for cross-modal temporal fusion established herein provides a replicable template for researchers developing diagnostic AI systems in LMICs. 1.5 Scope and Delimitation This study encompasses the design, development, and empirical evaluation of the  NeuroGuard  system across all stages of the software development lifecycle, from requirements elicitation and architectural design through implementation, testing, and user evaluation. The research is bounded by the following scope and delimitations. In terms of diagnostic scope, the system targets six classification categories: Parkinson's disease, early-stage Alzheimer's disease, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, post-stroke cognitive impairment, and healthy control. These conditions were selected based on their prevalence in the Philippine context, the availability of multi-modal biomarkers amenable to non-invasive measurement, and the clinical urgency of improved early detection. Other neurological conditions, including traumatic brain injury, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, Huntington's disease, and psychiatric comorbidities, are outside the scope of the present system, though the framework is extensible to accommodate additional categories with appropriate training data. With respect to data, the study draws on anonymized patient records from three partnering regional hospitals in the SOCCSKSARGEN region of Mindanao, collected between January 2022 and December 2023. The dataset encompasses 1,240 patient records, each comprising at least one valid 20-minute resting-state EEG recording, a minimum of five minutes of wearable IMU gait data, and a structured 15-minute cognitive interview transcript. Records lacking any of the three modalities were excluded from model training and evaluation but are discussed as a limitation. The technical delimitation of this study includes the exclusive use of non-invasive, outpatient-compatible data collection modalities. Neuroimaging modalities such as MRI and PET, while highly informative, require infrastructure and costs that are incompatible with the resource-constrained deployment contexts targeted by this study. Similarly, blood-based biomarker analysis, though increasingly relevant for Alzheimer's disease diagnosis, is not included in the present system due to the need for laboratory infrastructure and the longer turnaround times involved. The usability evaluation was conducted exclusively with healthcare professionals at the three partner hospitals and does not extend to community health workers or patients, who  represent important end-user groups for future evaluation studies. The clinical impact assessment is based on simulated workflow scenarios rather than prospective clinical trial data, and the system has not yet received regulatory clearance from the Food and Drug Administration of the Philippines, limiting its current use to research and demonstration purposes. 1.6 Definition of Terms For clarity and precision in the interpretation of this study, the following key terms are defined as they are used within this document. Artificial Intelligence (AI): The simulation of human cognitive processes, including learning, reasoning, and problem-solving, by computer systems. In this study, AI refers specifically to machine learning and deep learning models applied to  biosignal  analysis and diagnostic classification. Electroencephalogram (EEG): A neurophysiological test that records electrical activity generated by the brain through electrodes placed on the scalp. EEG data is analyzed in this study for frequency-band features indicative of specific neurological conditions. Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU): A compact electronic device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and the orientation of the body using a combination of accelerometers, gyroscopes, and magnetometers. In this study, IMUs embedded in wearable ankle and waist sensors capture gait kinematics. Multimodal Fusion: The computational process of combining information from multiple heterogeneous data sources into a unified representation for joint analysis. In this study, multimodal fusion refers to the integration of EEG, gait, and transcript data within the MTFT architecture. Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer (MTFT): The novel deep learning architecture proposed in this study, designed to learn cross-modal temporal dependencies among asynchronous  biosignal  streams through a hierarchical attention mechanism. Neurological Disorder: A disease or condition that involves the central and/or peripheral nervous system and disrupts normal neurological function. The six categories targeted in this study are Parkinson's disease, early-stage Alzheimer's disease, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, post-stroke cognitive impairment, and healthy control. System Usability Scale (SUS): A validated, industry-standard ten-item Likert scale questionnaire used to assess the perceived usability of interactive systems. SUS scores range from 0 to 100, with scores above 68 generally considered above average. Transfer Learning: A machine learning technique wherein a model trained on a large source dataset is adapted to a smaller target dataset by fine-tuning some or all of its parameters, leveraging representations learned in the source domain. Used in this study for transcript embedding and EEG feature initialization. Chapter 2 REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE 2.1 Global Burden of Neurological Disorders The epidemiological trajectory of neurological disorders over the past three decades represents one of the most significant and underappreciated public health transitions of the modern era. The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Study 2019, published by Lancet Neurology, documented that neurological disorders collectively accounted for 11.6% of global DALYs and 16.8% of all deaths worldwide, positioning them as the leading cause of disability in absolute terms. Within this category, stroke, Alzheimer's disease and other dementias, Parkinson's disease, epilepsy, and multiple sclerosis collectively account for the largest share of neurological burden, though the relative distribution varies substantially by region, income level, and demographic composition. Parkinson's disease has witnessed the most dramatic absolute increase in global prevalence of any neurological condition over the past 25 years. Dorsey et al. (2018) characterized this phenomenon as a 'Parkinson's pandemic,' noting that the number of people affected more than doubled from 2.5 million in 1990 to 6.1 million in 2016, and projections based on demographic aging suggest this figure will reach 12.9 million by 2040. While aging is the dominant risk factor, emerging evidence implicates pesticide exposure, industrial solvent contamination, and air pollution as contributors to incidence trends, factors with particular relevance to agricultural regions of the Philippines, where organophosphate pesticide use remains prevalent. Alzheimer's disease and other dementias collectively constitute the largest single cause of disability among older adults. The World Alzheimer Report 2023 estimated global dementia prevalence at 55 million, with 60-70% of cases attributable to Alzheimer's pathology. The economic cost of dementia care was estimated at USD 1.3 trillion annually, a figure projected to exceed USD 2.8 trillion by 2030 as demographic aging accelerates across Asia and Latin America. In Southeast Asia specifically, population aging is occurring at a rate unprecedented in human history; countries including Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, and the Philippines are  transitioning from young to aged societies in half the time this transition required in Western Europe, dramatically compressing the window available for healthcare system adaptation. Epilepsy presents a distinct epidemiological profile, characterized by high prevalence across all age groups and a disproportionate burden in LMICs, where an estimated 80% of the world's people with epilepsy reside. The 'treatment gap' in LMICs, defined as the proportion of people with active epilepsy who do not receive adequate treatment, reaches 75-95% in some settings, compared to less than 10% in high-income countries. In the Philippines, the Philippine Epilepsy Society estimates that only approximately 30% of the estimated 800,000 Filipinos with epilepsy are under consistent medical management, with the remainder either undiagnosed, untreated, or receiving inadequate pharmacotherapy. The psychological, social, and economic sequelae of neurological disorders extend far beyond the individual patient. Caregivers of patients with Alzheimer's disease or Parkinson's disease report substantially elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and burnout. Employment disruption affects not only the patient but frequently one or more family members who assume caregiving responsibilities. In the Philippine context, where extended family structures typically absorb caregiving functions, the labor market impact of neurological disorders is diffuse but substantial. A 2021 study commissioned by the Philippine Institute for Development Studies estimated that the indirect economic cost of neurological disorders, including lost productivity and informal care provision, amounted to PHP 47 billion annually. 2.2 Traditional Diagnostic Methods and Their Limitations The diagnostic evaluation of neurological disorders in current clinical practice encompasses a range of modalities, each with distinct strengths, limitations, and resource requirements. A brief survey of these methods establishes the baseline against which AI-augmented approaches must be assessed. The neurological clinical examination, consisting of a structured history-taking and systematic assessment of mental status, cranial nerves, motor function, coordination, reflexes, and sensory systems, remains the cornerstone of neurological diagnosis. Conducted by a trained neurologist, the clinical examination can identify patterns consistent with specific diagnoses and guide the selection of confirmatory investigations. However, the examination's  utility is fundamentally constrained by the availability of neurological expertise. In the absence of a specialist, generalist physicians have been shown to miss subtle signs of early Parkinson's disease, frontal lobe dysfunction, and absence epilepsy at rates that are clinically significant. Neuroimaging, encompassing structural MRI, functional MRI (fMRI), CT scanning, and PET imaging, provides invaluable information about brain structure and metabolism. MRI is now considered essential for the initial workup of dementia, movement disorders, and multiple sclerosis, enabling visualization of hippocampal atrophy, white matter lesions, and iron deposition patterns that are characteristic of specific conditions. However, MRI scanners cost between PHP 20 million and PHP 80 million and require specialized maintenance infrastructure. The density of MRI facilities in the Philippines is approximately 1.2 per million population, compared to 54 per million in Japan and 38 per million in the United States. Wait times for MRI at public hospitals in Manila frequently exceed three months, and in provincial areas, access may require patient transfer to a distant tertiary center. Electroencephalography, as noted in Chapter 1, provides the highest temporal resolution of any neuroimaging technique and remains indispensable for the diagnosis and classification of epileptic disorders. Standard EEG recording involves 19 or more scalp electrodes placed according to the international 10-20 system, with recordings typically spanning 20-40 minutes for routine studies and 24-72 hours for prolonged monitoring. Long-term EEG monitoring in video-EEG units provides the gold standard for epilepsy localization but requires 24-hour supervision by trained EEG technologists and neurophysiologists, a requirement virtually impossible to meet outside of a small number of Philippine tertiary hospitals. Even standard routine EEG interpretation is severely backlogged; a 2020 audit at a major Manila teaching hospital found an average of 18 days between EEG recording and neurologist report issuance. Neuropsychological assessment, encompassing standardized batteries of cognitive tests evaluating memory, attention, language, executive function, and visuospatial processing, provides quantitative characterization of cognitive decline critical for the diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease and post-stroke cognitive impairment. Standard comprehensive neuropsychological assessment requires 4-8 hours of testing by a trained neuropsychologist or psychometrician. In the Philippines, licensed  neuropsychologists  number in the hundreds, and  the cost of a comprehensive neuropsychological evaluation ranges from PHP 15,000 to PHP 40,000 at private institutions, with limited availability in the public sector. Genetic testing has emerged as a valuable adjunct in cases of suspected familial Parkinson's disease (LRRK2, SNCA mutations) and early-onset Alzheimer's disease (APOE genotyping, PSEN1/PSEN2 mutations). However, the clinical utility of routine genetic testing remains debated, and the infrastructure for genetic counseling and testing is extremely limited in the Philippine healthcare system. 2.3 Machine Learning in Medical Diagnosis: A Review The application of machine learning to medical diagnosis has a history spanning over four decades, from early expert systems and decision tree-based clinical decision support tools of the 1970s and 1980s to the contemporary deep learning revolution inaugurated by the breakthrough performance of  AlexNet  on ImageNet in 2012. The past decade has witnessed an explosive proliferation of ML-based diagnostic tools across virtually every medical specialty, driven by the convergence of large biomedical datasets, increased computational power, and algorithmic innovations in deep learning. In the domain of radiology and medical imaging, deep learning systems have achieved performance comparable to or exceeding that of board-certified specialist physicians on tasks including detection of diabetic retinopathy from fundus photographs, identification of pneumonia from chest radiographs, characterization of skin lesions from  dermoscopic  images, and detection of intracranial hemorrhage from CT scans. Landmark studies by  Esteva  et al. (2017) in Nature and  Rajpurkar  et al. (2018) in Nature Medicine established the credibility of deep learning as a clinical-grade diagnostic technology, catalyzing enormous investment in healthcare AI research and commercialization. The theoretical foundations underpinning modern medical AI are grounded in several key architectural paradigms. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have demonstrated particular effectiveness for spatial pattern recognition tasks, including medical image analysis, where local feature hierarchies (edges, textures, shapes) are informative for diagnosis. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), particularly Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs), are well-suited to sequential, time-series data such as EEG  signals and physiological monitoring traces, where temporal dependencies across time steps carry diagnostic information. More recently, the Transformer architecture introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017) and its successors (BERT, GPT,  ViT ) have displaced RNNs as the state-of-the-art architecture for sequence modeling tasks by virtue of their scalable self-attention mechanism, which enables modeling of arbitrary-range dependencies without the gradient flow limitations of recurrent architectures. A critical challenge in medical AI that has received increasing attention is the problem of algorithmic bias and generalization. Most published AI diagnostic systems have been developed and validated exclusively on patient populations from high-income countries, predominantly of European descent. When applied to genetically and demographically distinct populations in Asia, Africa, or Latin America, these systems frequently exhibit degraded performance, sometimes by clinically significant margins. Obermeyer et al. (2019) demonstrated in a landmark Science publication that a widely deployed commercial healthcare algorithm systematically underestimated the health needs of Black patients relative to White patients with equal clinical severity, perpetuating rather than reducing health disparities. This finding underscores the critical importance of developing and validating AI diagnostic tools on locally representative datasets, as this study endeavors to do. 2.4 EEG-Based Neural Disorder Classification The classification of neurological conditions from EEG signals has been an active area of computational neuroscience and signal processing research for over three decades. Early approaches relied on hand-engineered frequency-domain features extracted from the canonical EEG frequency bands: delta (0.5-4 Hz), theta (4-8 Hz), alpha (8-13 Hz), beta (13-30 Hz), and gamma (30-100 Hz), which each have known functional correlates and pathological significance. Classical machine learning classifiers, including support vector machines (SVMs), random forests, and k-nearest neighbors, trained on these spectral features, achieved early successes in binary classification tasks such as seizure versus non-seizure detection. The application of deep learning to EEG classification has dramatically expanded the performance envelope and scope of automated EEG analysis.  LeCun -inspired 1D and 2D CNN architectures applied to raw EEG waveforms or time-frequency spectrograms have  demonstrated strong performance for seizure detection, reaching sensitivities exceeding 95% on benchmark datasets such as the CHB-MIT Scalp EEG Database. LSTM-based models have been applied to model the temporal evolution of EEG dynamics across extended recording periods, capturing ictal-interictal state transitions that are characteristic of epileptic activity. For Alzheimer's disease diagnosis from EEG, research groups including those at the University of Toronto, Jena University Hospital, and the Center for Advanced Intelligence Project in Japan have developed CNN and Transformer-based classifiers trained on quantitative EEG ( qEEG ) features, achieving diagnostic accuracies of 88-93% for binary AD versus healthy control classification. Key EEG biomarkers of Alzheimer's disease include diffuse slowing of background rhythms (reduced alpha peak frequency), reduced coherence between frontal and temporal electrodes, and increased theta and delta power, all of which reflect the global cortical network disruption characteristic of the disease. Parkinson's disease, though primarily a disease of subcortical dopaminergic nuclei, produces measurable cortical EEG signatures including excessive beta-band oscillations in motor cortex, reduced event-related desynchronization during voluntary movement, and altered functional connectivity patterns. Seer et al. (2017) demonstrated that an SVM classifier trained on functional connectivity features extracted from resting-state EEG could distinguish Parkinson's patients from healthy controls with 84% accuracy, a result subsequently improved to 91% by  Yuvaraj  et al. (2021) using deep convolutional features from  spectro -temporal maps. Temporal segmentation and artifact management are critical preprocessing challenges in EEG-based AI. Ocular artifacts arising from blinks and eye movements, electromyographic contamination from muscle activity, and electrode impedance fluctuations can substantially corrupt EEG signals and introduce systematic biases into classifiers trained without adequate artifact rejection. The most widely adopted artifact rejection pipeline for research applications involves independent component analysis (ICA) followed by semi-automated component classification, though supervised deep learning approaches for artifact detection have shown promise as fully automated alternatives. 2.5 Gait Analysis and Wearable Sensors in Neurological Assessment Human gait, the pattern and mechanics of walking, serves as a rich and accessible biomarker of neurological and musculoskeletal health. The recognition that gait disturbances are early, sensitive, and quantifiable indicators of neurological pathology has motivated a substantial body of research into automated gait analysis over the past two decades. Gait abnormalities are prominent clinical features of Parkinson's disease (shuffling gait, reduced arm swing, festination, freezing of gait), normal pressure hydrocephalus (broad-based, magnetic gait), multiple sclerosis (ataxic or spastic gait), and stroke (hemiplegic gait), each exhibiting a distinct kinematic and temporal signature. Traditional clinical gait assessment relies on observational scoring scales such as the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS) Part III motor subscale or the Tinetti Gait and Balance Assessment. While standardized and widely used, these scales are subject to significant inter-rater variability, training effects, and limited sensitivity to subtle early-stage changes. Laboratory-based three-dimensional motion capture systems, employing retroreflective markers and an array of optical tracking cameras, provide gold-standard kinematic measurements but require expensive infrastructure, controlled laboratory conditions, and technical expertise that limits their clinical applicability. The development of miniaturized, low-cost inertial measurement units (IMUs) has democratized quantitative gait analysis by enabling ambulatory, in-the-wild gait monitoring without laboratory constraints. IMUs embedded in smartphones, dedicated wearable devices, or shoe insoles can measure linear acceleration and angular velocity during ambulation, from which a comprehensive suite of  spatio -temporal gait parameters including cadence, stride length, step time variability, trunk sway, and gait symmetry indices can be derived through validated algorithms. The APDM Mobility Lab, the  Physilog  system, and open-source platforms including the  GaitPy  Python package have established methodological standards for IMU-based gait parameter extraction. Machine learning applied to IMU gait data has demonstrated impressive diagnostic performance for Parkinson's disease. Studies by  Maetzler  et al. (2013) and  Mazilu  et al. (2016) established that ensemble learning methods trained on step-level IMU features could distinguish Parkinson's from healthy gait with accuracies of 87-94%. More recent work by El- Gohary  et al. (2020) demonstrated that deep learning models applied to raw IMU time series,  bypassing the feature engineering stage entirely, achieved a sensitivity of 96% and specificity of 93% for the detection of freezing of gait episodes, a clinically critical event that poses significant fall risk. The incorporation of gait analysis into a multimodal neurological assessment framework is theoretically motivated by its complementarity with EEG-based measures. While EEG captures cortical neural dynamics that may be altered in early disease prior to overt motor symptoms, gait provides a direct measure of the functional motor output of the nervous system that is sensitive to subcortical and cerebellar pathology. The combination of both modalities is therefore expected to yield superior diagnostic performance compared to either alone, particularly at the challenging early stages of disease when either modality in isolation may yield equivocal results. 2.6 Natural Language Processing in Clinical Assessment The incorporation of natural language processing (NLP) techniques into clinical decision support represents one of the most rapidly evolving frontiers of healthcare AI. Clinical text, encompassing physician notes, patient interview transcripts, discharge summaries, radiology reports, and structured assessment data, constitutes the largest and most information-rich data modality in healthcare, yet has historically been the least amenable to automated analysis due to its unstructured nature, technical vocabulary, and contextual complexity. The advent of large pre-trained language models, particularly BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers), introduced by Devlin et al. (2018), and its successors,  RoBERTa ,  BioBERT , and  ClinicalBERT , has fundamentally transformed the NLP capabilities available to medical AI researchers. These models, pre-trained on massive corpora of medical literature, clinical notes, and general text, provide rich contextualized word embeddings that capture semantic relationships far more sophisticated than earlier bag-of-words or  tf-idf  representations. Fine-tuned on task-specific clinical datasets, these models have achieved state-of-the-art performance across a range of clinical NLP tasks including clinical concept extraction, medication information extraction, clinical note classification, and question answering from electronic health records. For neurological assessment specifically, the analysis of spontaneous speech and structured cognitive interview transcripts has emerged as a promising diagnostic biomarker source. Patients with Alzheimer's disease exhibit characteristic linguistic changes including reduced lexical richness, increased repetition, more frequent indefinite references, shorter utterance length, and reduced syntactic complexity, patterns that emerge years before formal cognitive impairment thresholds are crossed on standardized tests. Fraser et al. (2016) demonstrated that NLP features extracted from picture description task transcripts could discriminate Alzheimer's disease from healthy controls with 81.9% accuracy, a result substantially improved by Luz et al. (2021) to 89.6% using pre-trained Transformer embeddings in the  DementiaBank  Pitt Corpus challenge. In this study, the NLP component of  NeuroGuard  analyzes transcripts from a structured 15-minute cognitive interview protocol adapted from the Montreal Cognitive Assessment ( MoCA ), the Addenbrooke's Cognitive Examination III (ACE-III), and the Alzheimer's Disease-8 (AD8) caregiver interview. The interview covers domains of orientation, memory recall, language fluency, verbal reasoning, and attention, with responses transcribed using a validated automated speech recognition (ASR) pipeline. Embeddings generated from  ClinicalBERT  fine-tuned on the  NeuroGuard  interview corpus are passed to the MTFT fusion module as the linguistic modality input. 2.7 Multimodal Deep Learning Architectures The problem of multimodal fusion, the principled combination of information from heterogeneous data sources into a unified representation for joint inference, has attracted sustained theoretical and empirical attention from the machine learning research community. Three broad categories of fusion strategy have been explored in the literature: early fusion, late fusion, and intermediate (or hybrid) fusion. Early fusion, also termed feature-level fusion, involves concatenating raw features or low-level representations from all modalities into a single joint feature vector prior to feeding into a classifier. While computationally simple, early fusion is vulnerable to the curse of dimensionality when features from individual modalities are high-dimensional, and it fails to capture modality-specific patterns that are better learned within separate processing streams.  Additionally, early fusion requires all modalities to be simultaneously available, making it brittle in clinical settings where one or more data sources may be missing or of poor quality for individual patients. Late fusion, or decision-level fusion, involves training independent classifiers for each modality and combining their output predictions, typically through weighted averaging, product of experts, or learned ensemble methods. Late fusion is robust to missing modalities (the absent modality's classifier can be down-weighted or excluded) and preserves modality-specific representational learning, but it cannot model cross-modal interactions during the feature learning process, limiting its ability to capture synergistic information that arises from the joint consideration of multiple modalities. Intermediate fusion, which processes each modality through a modality-specific encoder and then combines intermediate representations through learned fusion mechanisms, balances the advantages of early and late fusion. The precise architecture of the fusion mechanism is the critical design choice in intermediate fusion. Attention-based fusion, in which one modality's representation is used to attend to relevant portions of another modality's representation, has emerged as the most powerful and interpretable approach, directly analogous to the attention mechanisms underlying Transformer models for language and vision tasks. The Transformer architecture introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017) and its extension to multimodal contexts through models such as  ViLBERT  (Lu et al., 2019), LXMERT (Tan and Bansal, 2019), and  MedFuse  (Hayat et al., 2022) has established the paradigm within which the MTFT architecture proposed in this study operates. The MTFT extends existing multimodal Transformer approaches in several key dimensions: it operates on three rather than two modalities, it incorporates explicit temporal alignment mechanisms for asynchronous time-series data, and it includes a patient demographic conditioning module that allows the model to adapt its attention patterns based on patient age, sex, and medical history. 2.8 Telemedicine Platforms in the Philippine Context The concept of telemedicine, the delivery of healthcare services remotely through information and communication technologies, is not new to the Philippines, which has a long  history of innovative healthcare delivery solutions necessitated by its geographically dispersed archipelagic topology. Early telehealth initiatives in the Philippines date to the 1990s, when the Philippine General Hospital and the University of the Philippines Manila College of Medicine piloted satellite-based teleconsultation services linking Manila specialists with provincial health units. The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically accelerated the adoption of telemedicine in the Philippines. Republic Act 11223, the Universal Health Care Act of 2019, had already established a mandate for the development of telehealth infrastructure as part of the national health system strengthening agenda, and the pandemic provided the urgency and political will to accelerate implementation. By mid-2020, the Department of Health had launched  HealthNow , a national telehealth platform, and PhilHealth had begun reimbursing teleconsultation services for the first time. The Philippine Medical Association reported a 300% increase in registered telehealth practitioners between March 2020 and December 2020. Neurological telemedicine, or tele-neurology, is a particularly well-suited application domain for remote healthcare delivery. Clinical history-taking, medication management, neuropsychological assessment, and even certain aspects of the neurological examination (assessment of facial symmetry, speech clarity, tremor visualization via high-definition video) can be effectively conducted via teleconference. A pilot program conducted by the Philippine Neurological Association's Tele-Neurology Task Force at three provincial hospitals in 2021 demonstrated that 78% of neurology consultations could be managed effectively via video conference without requiring in-person evaluation. Existing telemedicine platforms in the Philippines, however, are predominantly general-purpose consultation platforms that lack the integrated diagnostic AI capabilities and multimodal  biosignal  analysis that  NeuroGuard  proposes.  KonsultaMD ,  HealthNow , and  Healthway  Medical's telehealth service offer video consultation and prescription management but do not incorporate objective physiological data collection or AI-assisted diagnostic interpretation.  NeuroGuard  thus occupies a distinct and currently unfilled position in the Philippine digital health ecosystem, combining the accessibility of telemedicine with the diagnostic rigor of AI-augmented multimodal  biosignal  analysis. 2.9 Synthesis of Literature The review of related literature presented in this chapter establishes a clear and compelling case for the development of  NeuroGuard . The global burden of neurological disorders is increasing, the Philippine healthcare system lacks the specialist capacity and infrastructure to meet existing diagnostic needs, and existing AI-based neurological diagnostic tools are predominantly unimodal and developed without validation on Filipino patient populations. The literature review further establishes the technical foundations upon which  NeuroGuard  is built. Deep learning approaches to EEG classification, gait analysis, and clinical transcript analysis have individually demonstrated strong performance, and theoretical and empirical arguments support the expectation that multimodal fusion will yield substantially superior diagnostic accuracy compared to any single modality. The Transformer architecture, particularly in its multimodal extension, represents the current state of the art for sequence modeling and fusion tasks, providing the appropriate architectural foundation for the MTFT. What is conspicuously absent from the existing literature, and what this study directly addresses, is an integrated system that combines all three modalities within a single, coherent diagnostic framework, validated on a locally representative Philippine dataset, and delivered through a deployable, user-friendly clinical platform.  NeuroGuard  fills this gap and, in doing so, contributes both to the scientific literature on multimodal medical AI and to the practical challenge of improving neurological care equity in the Philippines. Chapter 3 METHODOLOGY 3.1 Research Design This study adopts a mixed-methods research design, combining quantitative model development and performance evaluation with qualitative usability assessment and stakeholder feedback analysis. The quantitative component follows a development-and-evaluation paradigm, in which the  NeuroGuard  system is systematically designed, implemented, and assessed against defined performance benchmarks. The qualitative component employs structured interviews and observational methods to capture the nuanced perceptions, preferences, and concerns of healthcare professionals who serve as the primary end users of the system. The software development methodology applied throughout the system development lifecycle is the Agile Scrum framework, adapted for the research context. Development proceeded through five two-week sprints, each organized around a specific system component: (1) data pipeline and preprocessing infrastructure, (2) individual modality encoders and feature extraction modules, (3) MTFT fusion architecture and model training, (4) web dashboard and mobile application development, and (5) integration testing, deployment, and evaluation. Sprint retrospectives and stakeholder review meetings informed iterative refinements at each stage, ensuring that evolving clinical requirements were incorporated into the system design. The research paradigm is fundamentally applied and interventionist in orientation: the primary aim is not the generation of abstract theoretical knowledge but the development of a practical technological solution to a concrete healthcare problem, assessed through empirical performance metrics and real-world usability testing. This orientation situates the study within the tradition of design science research, as articulated by  Hevner  et al. (2004), wherein the production of an artifact (in this case, the  NeuroGuard  system) is itself a research contribution, subject to evaluation against rigorously defined criteria of utility, quality, and fit to purpose. 3.2 System Architecture The  NeuroGuard  system architecture comprises four interconnected layers:  the  Data Acquisition Layer, the Signal Processing and Feature Extraction Layer, the Multimodal Inference Layer, and the Application and Presentation Layer. Each layer is described in detail below, with reference to the system architecture diagram presented as Figure 2. The Data Acquisition Layer encompasses three distinct data capture subsystems. The EEG subsystem consists of a portable, 32-channel wireless EEG headset connected via Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) to a tablet-based acquisition application, which streams raw EEG data at 512 Hz sampling rate to the  NeuroGuard  server via a secure HTTPS endpoint. The gait subsystem consists of two 9-degree-of-freedom IMU sensors (tri-axial accelerometer, gyroscope, and magnetometer) worn on the patient's dominant ankle and waist, sampling at 200 Hz. The cognitive interview subsystem comprises a structured interview protocol administered by a healthcare worker through a tablet application, with patient responses captured through the device microphone and transcribed in near-real-time using a fine-tuned Wav2Vec 2.0 ASR model. The Signal Processing and Feature Extraction Layer applies modality-specific preprocessing pipelines to the raw data streams received from the acquisition layer. Preprocessed features from each modality are encoded into fixed-dimension embedding vectors by modality-specific encoder networks, which serve as the inputs to the MTFT fusion module. Detailed descriptions of each preprocessing pipeline and encoder architecture are provided in Sections 3.4 and 3.5. The Multimodal Inference Layer centers on the MTFT architecture, which receives the three modality embeddings, applies cross-modal temporal attention, and produces a probability distribution over the six diagnostic categories. The MTFT output is supplemented by a SHAP-based feature attribution module that generates clinician-interpretable explanations of the diagnostic decision, identifying the specific features from each modality that most strongly contributed to the classification outcome. The inference pipeline is deployed as a containerized microservice using Docker and Kubernetes, enabling horizontal scaling to accommodate concurrent patient analyses without latency degradation. The Application and Presentation Layer consists of two client-facing interfaces. The  NeuroGuard  Clinical Dashboard is a React.js web application accessible through any modern browser, providing clinicians with real-time diagnostic reports, patient record management, longitudinal trend visualization, and system configuration capabilities. The  NeuroGuard  Mobile Companion is an Android application optimized for low-end devices (Android 9.0 or later, minimum 2 GB RAM) that enables patient-side data collection, self-reported symptom logging, medication reminder management, and asynchronous transmission of gait and interview data to the server for analysis when network connectivity is available. 3.3 Data Sources and Collection The primary dataset for this study was assembled through a formal data-sharing agreement with three regional hospitals in the SOCCSKSARGEN region of Mindanao: Southern Mindanao Regional Medical Center (SMRMC) in Davao, General Santos City Integrated Hospital (GSCIH), and  Koronadal  General Hospital (KGH). Ethical clearance for the study was obtained from the institutional review boards of all three hospitals and from the research ethics committee of the Mindanao Institute of Technology and Applied Sciences (MITAS), with protocol approval references SMRMC-REC-2021-041, GSCIH-REB-2021-017, KGH-REC-2022-008, and MITAS-REC-2021-112. Prospective data collection commenced in January 2022 and concluded in December 2023. Eligible participants included adult patients (18 years of age or older) presenting to the neurology outpatient clinics of the three hospitals with a confirmed or suspected neurological diagnosis in one of the five target disease categories, or age- and sex-matched healthy control volunteers recruited from hospital staff and the general community. Exclusion criteria included the presence of significant hearing or visual impairment precluding cognitive interview participation, implanted electronic devices contraindicated for EEG recording, inability to ambulate independently (for gait data collection), and clinical instability requiring urgent intervention at the time of enrollment. A total of 1,240 patients were enrolled and completed full trimodal data collection, comprising 289 patients with Parkinson's disease, 231 with early-stage Alzheimer's disease, 198 with epilepsy, 145 with multiple sclerosis, 177 with post-stroke cognitive impairment, and  200 healthy controls. All participants provided written informed consent prior to enrollment. Patient records were fully de-identified prior to transfer to the research team, with a secure key file maintained by a designated data custodian at each hospital. Data collection at each site followed a standardized protocol administered by trained research assistants who had completed a 40-hour protocol training program delivered by the research team. The standardized protocol covered EEG cap placement and impedance verification, IMU sensor placement and calibration, cognitive interview administration and recording, and data quality verification procedures. Inter-rater reliability of the protocol was assessed through double-data collection on 5% of subjects, yielding intraclass correlation coefficients above 0.92 for all quantitative gait parameters and Cohen's kappa above 0.85 for cognitive interview administration consistency. 3.4 Data Preprocessing The preprocessing of raw data from each of the three modalities involved distinct pipelines tailored to the specific signal characteristics and noise properties of each data type. The pipelines are described sequentially below. 3.4.1 EEG Preprocessing Pipeline Raw 32-channel EEG data was imported in European Data Format (EDF) and processed using the MNE-Python library (version 1.4). The preprocessing pipeline consisted of the following sequential stages. First, a fourth-order Butterworth bandpass filter with cutoff frequencies of 0.5 Hz and 70 Hz was applied to remove DC drift and high-frequency noise, followed by a 50 Hz notch filter to attenuate powerline interference. Second, the filtered signal was re-referenced to the common average reference. Third, Independent Component Analysis (ICA) using the  FastICA  algorithm was applied with 25 components; components corresponding to ocular artifacts (identified by frontal electrode topography and correlation with electro-oculographic channels), cardiac artifacts (identified by temporal correlation with R-peak annotations), and muscle artifacts (identified by high-frequency spectral profiles) were automatically labeled using the  ICALabel  classifier and subtracted from the signal. Fourth, the cleaned continuous EEG was  epoched  into 4-second non-overlapping segments, and epochs with residual amplitude exceeding 100 microvolts peak-to-peak in any channel were rejected.  For each accepted epoch, a 32x129 time-frequency representation was computed using short-time Fourier transform (STFT) with a Hann window, yielding a  spectro -temporal map spanning 0.5-64 Hz. These maps served as inputs to the EEG encoder. 3.4.2 Gait Preprocessing Pipeline IMU data from the ankle and waist sensors was processed using a custom Python pipeline built on top of the  GaitPy  and SciPy libraries. The pipeline applied a second-order Butterworth low-pass filter with a 20 Hz cutoff to remove high-frequency noise from the accelerometer channels. Gait event detection (initial and final contact events) was performed using the GSDB algorithm applied to the vertical acceleration channel of the ankle sensor. Detected strides were segmented and used to compute a 31-dimensional feature vector per stride, comprising: cadence, stride time, stride length estimate (computed from double integration of ankle acceleration with drift correction), stance and swing phase durations, stride time variability (coefficient of variation), step asymmetry index, trunk acceleration root mean square (RMS) in the vertical, mediolateral, and anteroposterior axes, harmonic ratio of trunk acceleration, and first five dominant frequency components of the mediolateral and anteroposterior trunk acceleration power spectral density. Stride features were aggregated across the 5-minute recording using mean, standard deviation, and percentile statistics, yielding a 93-dimensional patient-level feature vector for the gait modality. 3.4.3 Cognitive Interview Transcript Preprocessing Automated transcripts generated by the Wav2Vec 2.0 ASR system were manually reviewed by trained research assistants and corrected for transcription errors in 12% of cases where word error rate exceeded 10%, identified by reviewer flagging during quality control. Corrected transcripts were tokenized and encoded using the  ClinicalBERT  model (base configuration, 768-dimensional embeddings, 12 Transformer layers, pre-trained on 1.2 billion words of clinical text). For each interview response, the mean of  ClinicalBERT's  penultimate-layer token representations was computed as the sentence embedding. The embeddings of individual responses were concatenated with hand-crafted lexical features including type-token ratio (a measure of lexical richness), mean syntactic parse tree depth, utterance count, word count per utterance, and frequency of indefinite pronouns and pauses. This combined  representation was projected to a 256-dimensional interview embedding through a two-layer feedforward projection network trained end-to-end with the MTFT. 3.5 Model Development: MTFT Architecture The Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer (MTFT) is the central AI component of  NeuroGuard  and represents the novel methodological contribution of this study. Its architecture is motivated by the observation that neurological disorders produce temporally correlated signatures across EEG, gait, and cognitive modalities, and that explicitly modeling these temporal cross-modal correlations should yield superior diagnostic performance compared to modality-independent processing. The MTFT architecture comprises three stages: modality-specific encoding, cross-modal temporal attention, and diagnostic classification. In the modality-specific encoding stage, each preprocessed input is processed by a dedicated encoder network. The EEG encoder is a lightweight CNN-Transformer hybrid: a four-layer 2D CNN extracts spatial features from the  spectro -temporal maps of each epoch, and a 4-layer Transformer encoder  models  temporal dependencies across consecutive epochs within a recording session. The gait encoder is a two-layer bidirectional LSTM that processes the stride-level feature sequence chronologically, capturing within-session gait evolution patterns. The interview encoder is the  ClinicalBERT  projection network described in Section 3.4.3. Each encoder produces a sequence of d=256-dimensional embedding vectors, aligned to a common temporal resolution of 10-second windows through learned temporal interpolation. In the cross-modal temporal attention stage, the three modality-specific embedding sequences are processed by a three-way cross-attention mechanism. Each modality's embedding serves as the query in attention computations against the key-value pairs of the other two modalities, enabling the model to learn which aspects of each modality are most informative given the context provided by the other modalities. This cross-modal attention is applied over H=8 parallel attention heads, with each head potentially learning a distinct inter-modal interaction pattern. The cross-attention outputs are combined through a gating mechanism conditioned on a patient demographic embedding (age, sex, and medical history features), enabling personalized modality weighting. In the classification stage, the time-averaged cross-attended representation is passed through a three-layer feedforward network with GELU activation functions and a dropout rate of 0.3, culminating in a  softmax  output layer producing a probability distribution over the six diagnostic categories. The MTFT was implemented in  PyTorch  (version 2.0) and trained on NVIDIA A100 GPUs via a cloud computing grant. Training employed the  AdamW  optimizer with a cosine annealing learning rate schedule, an initial learning rate of 2e-4, a batch size of 32, and a maximum of 100 epochs with early stopping based on validation loss plateau. Class imbalance was addressed through weighted cross-entropy loss with class weights inversely proportional to class frequency. Data augmentation was applied to EEG and gait training data through random signal warping, Gaussian noise injection, and temporal masking. The model was evaluated using 5-fold stratified cross-validation, with patient-level splitting to prevent data leakage. 3.6 System Implementation The  NeuroGuard  software system was implemented across a heterogeneous technology stack, selected to balance performance, developer productivity, open-source availability, and compatibility with the Philippine healthcare IT environment. The backend server is implemented in Python 3.10 using the  FastAPI  framework, deployed on Docker containers orchestrated by Kubernetes on a private cloud infrastructure maintained by MITAS. The ML inference pipeline is served through Triton Inference Server, enabling efficient batched inference with GPU acceleration. Patient records are stored in a PostgreSQL relational database, with EEG and gait raw data archived in an S3-compatible object storage service. All data in transit is encrypted using TLS 1.3, and data at rest is encrypted using AES-256. Access control is implemented through role-based authentication with JSON Web Token (JWT) validation, with distinct access levels for administrators, attending physicians, and data collection personnel. The  NeuroGuard  Clinical Dashboard was implemented using React.js 18 with TypeScript, utilizing the  Recharts  and D3.js libraries for data visualization. The dashboard supports real-time EEG signal display through WebSocket streaming, interactive longitudinal  trend charts for key biomarkers, and a PDF report generation module for documentation and referral purposes. The dashboard was designed in accordance with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 AA standard and is optimized for screen resolutions common in Philippine hospital settings. The  NeuroGuard  Mobile Companion was developed using the Flutter framework (Dart language), enabling a single codebase deployable on Android and iOS platforms. The application implements an offline-first architecture using SQLite local storage and background synchronization, ensuring usability in areas with intermittent internet connectivity. IMU data collection is implemented through the Flutter Sensors Plus plugin, supporting all major Android device sensor chipsets. The application was tested for compatibility with 22 Android device models spanning Android 9-14, including low-cost devices ( Realme  C35, Samsung Galaxy A03, Vivo Y02) commonly owned by patients in the study area. 3.7 Evaluation Framework The evaluation of  NeuroGuard  encompassed three complementary dimensions: model classification performance, system usability, and clinical workflow impact. Model classification performance was assessed using the held-out test set from 5-fold cross-validation, comprising 20% of the dataset (248 patient records). Performance metrics computed for each diagnostic category and in aggregate included accuracy, sensitivity (recall), specificity, positive predictive value (precision), F1-score, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC). Confidence intervals were computed using bootstrap resampling with 1,000 iterations. Ablation studies were conducted to quantify the marginal contribution of each modality by training and evaluating MTFT variants with progressively fewer modalities. System usability was evaluated through the System Usability Scale (SUS), a validated 10-item Likert questionnaire administered to 45 healthcare professionals (physicians, nurses, and allied health workers) from the three partner hospitals following a 2-hour structured orientation and hands-on session with the  NeuroGuard  system. Qualitative feedback was collected through 15-minute semi-structured interviews with a purposive subsample of 15 participants representing diverse roles, institutions, and levels of digital literacy. Interview  transcripts were analyzed using thematic analysis following the framework of Braun and Clarke (2006). Clinical workflow impact was assessed through a structured simulation study in which 12 neurology clinicians were asked to complete 20 standardized case assessments each under two conditions: (1) standard-of-care assessment without AI support and (2) AI-augmented assessment using  NeuroGuard . The primary outcome was time-to-diagnosis (elapsed time from data collection to documented diagnostic decision). Secondary outcomes included clinician confidence ratings, inter-rater agreement on diagnostic category, and rates of referral for additional specialist evaluation. Chapter 5 SUMMARY, CONCLUSION, AND RECOMMENDATION 5.1 Summary This study was undertaken in response to the critical and growing gap between the burden of neurological disorders in the Philippines and the capacity of the Philippine healthcare system to provide timely, accurate, and equitable neurological diagnosis. Grounded in the recognition that artificial intelligence, particularly deep multimodal learning, offers a transformative opportunity to bridge this gap, the study designed, developed, and rigorously evaluated  NeuroGuard : an AI-powered, multimodal system for the early detection and longitudinal monitoring of neurological disorders. The specific neurological conditions targeted by  NeuroGuard  are Parkinson's disease, early-stage Alzheimer's disease, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, post-stroke cognitive impairment, and healthy control status. The system integrates three complementary data modalities: electroencephalographic (EEG) signals captured through a portable wireless headset, gait kinematics measured through wearable inertial measurement unit (IMU) sensors, and cognitive function assessed through structured interview transcripts analyzed by natural language processing techniques. The integration of these modalities is achieved through a novel deep learning architecture, the Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer (MTFT), which employs cross-modal temporal attention mechanisms to learn the joint diagnostic information contained in the combination of these modalities. The development methodology followed the Agile Scrum framework, with the system built across five development sprints encompassing data pipeline construction, modality-specific encoder development, MTFT architecture implementation, web and mobile application development, and system integration and evaluation. The dataset used for model training and evaluation comprised 1,240 anonymized patient records from three regional hospitals in the SOCCSKSARGEN region of Mindanao, Philippines, collected over a 24-month period between January 2022 and December 2023. The system was evaluated across three dimensions. First, the MTFT model achieved an overall classification accuracy of 94.7%, with a sensitivity of 96.2%, specificity of 93.1%, and an aggregate AUROC of 0.975 across the six diagnostic categories. These results represent a substantial and statistically significant improvement over single-modality baselines and published multimodal approaches. Ablation studies confirmed that each of the three modalities contributes meaningfully to overall performance, with EEG providing the highest single-modality value, followed by gait analysis and cognitive interview features. Second, the usability evaluation conducted with 45 healthcare professionals from the three partner hospitals yielded a mean System Usability Scale score of 82.4, indicative of 'Good' usability and approaching the 'Excellent' threshold. Qualitative analysis of semi-structured interviews identified themes of diagnostic empowerment and improved clinical confidence as the dominant positive user responses, alongside actionable suggestions for interface simplification and enhanced offline functionality. Third, the clinical workflow impact simulation demonstrated that  NeuroGuard -augmented assessment reduced mean time-to-diagnosis from 14.3 hours to 2.1 hours, increased clinician confidence and inter-rater agreement, and reduced unnecessary specialist referral rates by 22.5 percentage points. Together, these findings provide strong empirical support for the potential of  NeuroGuard  to improve neurological diagnostic capacity in resource-constrained Philippine healthcare settings and to serve as a model for multimodal AI-augmented diagnostic systems in other healthcare contexts. 5.2 Conclusion Based on the findings of this study, the researchers draw the following conclusions. First, the Multimodal Temporal Fusion Transformer architecture proposed in this study demonstrates that the integration of EEG, gait, and cognitive transcript data within a unified cross-modal attention framework yields substantially superior neurological disorder classification performance compared to single-modality or simple late-fusion baselines. The 94.7% overall classification accuracy achieved on a locally validated Filipino patient dataset is not only statistically significant but clinically meaningful, approaching the reported inter-rater agreement of trained neurologists on comparable diagnostic tasks. This establishes  multimodal deep learning as a viable and highly promising approach for automated neurological disorder detection. Second, the development of a locally curated and validated dataset of Filipino neurological patient  biosignals  addresses a critical gap in the medical AI literature and provides a foundation for future research that is not dependent on population-transferred models potentially misaligned with the genetic, demographic, and epidemiological characteristics of Filipino patients. The dataset itself constitutes a significant and independently valuable research contribution that will be made available to the broader research community through a controlled data-sharing mechanism. Third, the strong usability performance of  NeuroGuard  among healthcare professionals of diverse specializations and varying levels of digital literacy, confirmed by both quantitative SUS scores and qualitative interview findings, suggests that the system is sufficiently intuitive and well-designed for real-world clinical deployment. Critically, the clinician-interpretable SHAP-based explanations provided by the system play an important role in building user trust, confirming that the design decision to prioritize  explainability  alongside accuracy was well-founded. Fourth, the dramatic reduction in time-to-diagnosis and the improvement in inter-rater agreement documented in the clinical workflow simulation provide compelling evidence that  NeuroGuard , if deployed at scale, would produce tangible improvements in diagnostic efficiency and standardization within the Philippine neurological care system. The potential to reduce unnecessary specialist referrals by approximately 22 percentage points is particularly significant given the scarcity of specialist neurological capacity in the country. In aggregate, this study concludes that  NeuroGuard  is a technically sound, usable, and clinically impactful system that is ready for a prospective pilot deployment in real-world Philippine clinical settings, subject to completion of the regulatory clearance process. The researchers are confident that  NeuroGuard  represents a meaningful and timely contribution to the mission of health equity in the Philippines. 5.3 Recommendation On the basis of the findings, conclusions, and limitations of this study, the researchers advance the following recommendations addressed to distinct stakeholder groups. For future researchers and developers, the researchers recommend the following avenues for extending and improving upon the work presented in this study. First, the incorporation of additional data modalities, particularly retinal imaging (which has emerged as a promising non-invasive window into brain neurodegeneration through the optic nerve) and voice acoustic analysis (capturing prosody, speech rate, and vocal tremor features distinct from the transcript content already included), should be explored as potential complements to the existing three-modality MTFT framework. Second, the validation dataset should be expanded through collaborative partnerships with hospitals in additional Philippine regions, including the National Capital Region, the Cordillera Administrative Region, and the Eastern Visayas, to enhance the geographic and demographic representativeness of the training data and improve model generalizability. Third, prospective longitudinal studies with follow-up periods of 24-36 months are required to establish the clinical validity of  NeuroGuard  as an early detection tool, by determining whether  NeuroGuard's  diagnostic signals precede the onset of clinically recognized symptoms in participants initially classified as healthy controls or as early-stage disease. For healthcare institutions and administrators, the researchers recommend the establishment of pilot deployment programs at public provincial hospitals in underserved regions as the immediate next step. Pilot programs should be accompanied by structured training curricula for clinical staff, clear protocols for integrating  NeuroGuard  outputs into clinical decision-making workflows, and independent clinical audit mechanisms to monitor system performance and safety in real-world conditions. Hospital administrators should engage with PhilHealth to explore pathways for  NeuroGuard -augmented assessments to be included in the benefit package for neurological consultation, which would be a critical step toward ensuring equitable access for low-income patients. For the Department of Health and relevant government agencies, the researchers recommend prioritizing the development of a regulatory pathway for AI-augmented medical diagnostic systems at the Food and Drug Administration of the Philippines, in alignment with international best practices established by the US FDA, EU MDR, and MHLW of Japan. The  current absence of a clearly defined AI medical device regulatory framework creates uncertainty that slows the adoption of beneficial technologies like  NeuroGuard . The DOH should also consider incorporating AI-augmented neurological screening into the Universal Health Care implementation roadmap, particularly as an adjunct to the network of primary care benefit providers being established under RA 11223. For the Philippine Neurological Association and academic medical institutions, the researchers recommend the development of continuing professional education programs that prepare neurologists and allied health professionals for effective collaboration with AI diagnostic tools. The qualitative findings of this study suggest that user trust and system acceptance are substantially enhanced when clinicians understand the basic principles underlying the model's diagnostic reasoning. Structured AI health literacy programs embedded in neurology residency training and continuing medical education curricula would provide the professional workforce readiness necessary for sustained, effective AI system deployment. Finally, for future capstone and thesis researchers in information technology and related disciplines, the researchers recommend this study as a model demonstrating that high-quality, clinically meaningful research can be conducted within the constraints of an undergraduate capstone project when it is grounded in genuine healthcare needs, conducted with appropriate ethical safeguards, and subject to rigorous empirical evaluation. The  NeuroGuard  codebase, dataset access protocol, and system documentation will be archived and made available through the MITAS Institutional Repository as a resource for future student researchers. APPENDICES Appendix A: EEG Data Collection Protocol (Excerpt) The following protocol was used by research assistants for standardized EEG data collection across all three partner hospital sites. The complete protocol document (version 3.2, approved March 2022) is archived at MITAS and available from the corresponding researcher upon request. A.1 Pre-Recording Participant Preparation. Upon arrival to the EEG recording room, the participant is asked to remove any earrings, hairpins, or hearing aids that may interfere with electrode placement. The research assistant explains the procedure in the participant's preferred language (Filipino, Cebuano, Hiligaynon, or English) and answers any questions before proceeding. Participants are asked to avoid caffeine consumption for at least four hours prior to recording, minimize scalp product use (gels, sprays) on the day of recording, and ensure adequate sleep (at least 7 hours) the night before, conditions that are confirmed through a brief pre-recording checklist. A.2 Electrode Placement. Electrodes are placed according to the international 10-20 system using the 32-channel standard clinical montage. Electrode impedances are verified to be below 5  kΩ  for all channels before recording commences. Channels with impedances exceeding 5  kΩ  after three attempts at impedance reduction (gentle scalp abrasion with  NuPrep  gel) are noted in the recording metadata and excluded from analysis during preprocessing. A.3 Recording Procedure. The 20-minute resting-state recording protocol consists of four conditions administered in fixed order: (1) 5 minutes eyes closed, (2) 2 minutes eyes open, (3) 5 minutes eyes closed, (4) 3 minutes visual stimulation (checkerboard pattern at 8 Hz), (5) 5 minutes eyes closed. The participant is instructed to remain still, minimize jaw clenching, blink normally during eyes-open conditions, and avoid deliberate eye movements. The research assistant monitors signal quality in real time and annotates any participant movements, external noises, or technical events that may contaminate the recording. Appendix B: Cognitive Interview Protocol The  NeuroGuard  Cognitive Interview (NGCI) is a structured 15-minute assessment developed specifically for this study by adapting validated items from the Montreal Cognitive Assessment ( MoCA ), the Addenbrooke's Cognitive Examination III (ACE-III), and the Verbal Fluency Test. The NGCI consists of six sections administered sequentially. Section 1: Orientation (2 minutes). The interviewer asks the participant to state the current date (day, month, year, day of week), current time (to the nearest hour), current location (city, institution), and country and head of state. Responses are recorded verbatim. Section 2: Immediate and Delayed Memory (4 minutes). The interviewer reads a list of 10 common nouns at a rate of one per second and asks the participant to immediately recall as many as possible. After 10 minutes of intervening tasks, the participant is asked to recall the same list without cue. Section 3: Verbal Fluency (2 minutes). The participant is asked to name as many animals as possible in 60 seconds, followed by as many words beginning with the letter 'P' as possible in 60 seconds. Section 4: Language and Visuospatial Reasoning (3 minutes). The participant is asked to describe in detail what is happening in a complex picture scene (the Cookie Theft picture from the Boston Diagnostic Aphasia Examination), to repeat three complex sentences, and to draw a clock showing 10 minutes past 11. Section 5: Attention and Working Memory (2 minutes). The participant is asked to perform serial subtractions from 100 by 7, and to repeat a sequence of digits forwards and backwards. Section 6: Caregiver Report (2 minutes). If a caregiver is present, three items adapted from the Alzheimer's Disease-8 are administered to the caregiver regarding changes in the patient's daily functioning observed over the past year.
